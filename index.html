Biographical: Together with Kyoshi Hasegawa and Yozo Hamaguchi one of the most significant practitioners of our times of the art of mezzotint (manière noire). Rudolf Bauer (1889-1953)

Biographical: A pioneering German-born artist who, along with Vasily Kandinsky, was among the first abstract artists in the world. In the 1930s Bauer replaced the raucous, organic symphony of shapes he had developed in the 1910s with a more balanced, geometric esthetic. In the first half of the 20th century, Bauer's works were displayed alongside the most renowned luminaries of modern art in numerous galleries and museums throughout the world: Modern European Art exhibition (1933) and Modern Works of Art (1934) at the Museum of Modern Art; an exhibition of Guggenheim's collection in Charleston (1936) and in his own solo show at the Arts Club of Chicago; a one-man exhibition at Jeu de Paume (1937) in Paris. Bauer's work was included in the Degenerate Art exhibition in Munich (1937)and Bauer himself was briefly incarcerated by the Nazis until he was released, thanks to the efforts of Solomon R. Guggenheim and Rebay's brother. Bauer moved to the United States in 1939. That same year, a Bauer painting appeared on the flyer advertising The Art of Tomorrow, the Museum of Non-Objective Painting's inaugural exhibition. During his time in the US, Bauer taught recipients of Guggenheim grants and was frequently included in group exhibitions of Guggenheim's collection. His “non-objective” abstract style would go on to influence virtually all aspects of modern art and serve as a beacon to Pollock, Rothko, and other artists who formed the New York school of Abstract Expressionism. However by the end of World War II, Bauer had stopped painting and fell into obscurity. Bauer died in his home in New Jersey in 1953. Postumous exhibitions of Bauer's work have been organized by numerous Galeries internationally starting in 1969. His influence on the formation of the Guggenheim collection and museum were acknowledged by that institution's publications in 2005.

Bernard Buffet (1928-1999)

Biographical: Was a French painter, printmaker, and sculptor. He produced a varied and extensive body of work. His style was exclusively figurative and is often classified as Expressionist or "miserabilist". As a painter, Buffet produced religious pieces, landscapes, portraits and still-lifes. Influenced by Francis Gruber, he often painted "Miserabilist" scenes of despair, including scenes of poverty and Holocaust victims, but he also portrayed subjects as varied as ashtrays, clowns, and table lamps. His work was characterized by thick black lines, elongated forms, and a lack of depth of field. An extremely prolific painter, he had at least one major exhibition every year. By the age 26, it was said that he had completed more paintings than Pierre-Auguste Renoir's lifetime output. In 1948, gallerist Maurice Garnier began showing Buffet's work, and by 1977, his gallery was devoted solely to Buffet. Buffet enjoyed worldwide popularity in the 1950s and was often compared to Picasso for his fame and talent. A 1958 article in The New York Times called him one of the "Fabulous Five" cultural figures of post-war France (the other four were Brigitte Bardot, Françoise Sagan, Roger Vadim, and Yves Saint Laurent). That same year, at the age of 30, the first retrospective of his work was held at the Galerie Charpentier. By the end of the 1950s, however, the public and art community turned strongly against him due to changing artistic tastes, Buffet's lavish lifestyle, and his extremely prolific output. The 21st century saw a renewed interest in his oeuvre. His work is particularly popular in Asia and former Soviet Union nations. In 2016, Paris's Musée d'Art Moderne held a large retrospective of his work, the first held in France since his death. Corresponding with this renewed interest, some of Buffet's work also saw rising appraisals in the early 21st century.

Max Ernst (1891-1976)

Biographical: was a German (naturalised American in 1948 and French in 1958) painter, sculptor, printmaker, graphic artist, and poet. He began painting in 1909 while studying at the University of Bonn, and later joined the Die Rheinischen Expressionisten group of artists. He served as a soldier for four years during World War I, and this experience left him shocked, traumatised and critical of the modern world. During World War II he was designated an "undesirable foreigner" while living in France. Ernst's work often featured ironic juxtapositions of grotesque elements with Cubist and Expressionist motifs. A prolific artist, Ernst was a primary pioneer of the Dada movement and Surrealism in Europe. He had no formal artistic training, but his experimental attitude toward the making of art resulted in his invention of frottage—a technique that uses pencil rubbings of textured objects and relief surfaces to create images—and grattage, an analogous technique in which paint is scraped across canvas to reveal the imprints of the objects placed beneath. Ernst is noted for his unconventional drawing methods as well as for creating novels and pamphlets using the method of collages. He had a fascination with birds, often including his alter ego, Loplop, a bird, in his work. He eventually settled in France and achieved financial success in the 1950s.

Edouard Manet (1858-1941)

Biographical: Was a French modernist painter. He was one of the first 19th-century artists to paint modern urban life, and was instrumental in its establishment as acceptable material for high art, as well as a pivotal figure in the transition from Realism to Impressionism. Manet was among the first artist to take serious risks with the public whose favour he sought. Although harshly condemned by critics who decried its lack of conventional subject and finish, Manet's work had admirers from the beginning. The roughly painted style and photographic lighting in Manet's paintings was seen as specifically modern, and as a challenge to the Renaissance works he copied or used as source material ( one of the first to take liberties with Renaissance perspective and to offer 'pure painting' as a source of aesthetic pleasure) . He rejected the technique he had learned in the studio of Thomas Couture - in which a painting was constructed using successive layers of paint on a dark-toned ground - in favor of a direct, alla prima method (the first to make alla prima painting the standard technique for oil painting) using opaque paint on a light ground. Novel at the time, this method made possible the completion of a painting in a single sitting. It was adopted by the Impressionists, and became the prevalent method of painting in oils for generations that followed. Manet's work is considered "early modern", partially because of the opaque flatness of his surfaces, the frequent sketch-like passages, and the black outlining of figures, all of which draw attention to the surface of the picture plane and the material quality of paint. Manet's public career lasted from 1861, the year of his first participation in the Salon, until his death in 1883. His known extant works, as catalogued in 1975 by Denis Rouart and Daniel Wildenstein, comprise 430 oil paintings, 89 pastels, and more than 400 works on paper.

Margarett Sargent (1892-1978)

Biographical: A noted painter in the Ashcan School. She was an unconventional member of an Aristocratic Boston family and fourth cousin of artist John Singer Sargent. She exhibited as Margarett Sargent and Margarett W. McKean. Sargent first exhibited as a sculptor in 1916 at the Art Institute of Chicago's annual exhibition. She was creating portraits of children and animals, but in 1917 decided to study with sculptor Gutzon Borglum of Mt. Rushmore fame. Through him, she met George Luks, a painter who became her artistic mentor. The sculptural portrait she did of Luks and entered in the Pennsylvania Academy annual show was praised by critics. Luks introduced her to John Kraushaar, a New York dealer whose Fifth Avenue gallery hosted Sargent's first solo exhibition in 1926. Lincoln Kirstein , founder of the New York City Ballet, gave her a solo exhibition at the Harvard Society for Contemporary Art, in a season that included Picasso and Matisse among other luminaries. She later had shows in Boston and Chicago eventually participating in as many as 30 shows. Her paintings have been described as "intense, bold, and expressive."The portraits she painted, which constituted a majority of her work, followed the school-of-Paris modernists and German Expressionists tendencies. The person was shown in a simplified form, but the face was highlighted and more decoratively painted. She stopped painting in 1936, when it had gotten "too intense" for her, as she told her granddaughter.

Adolf Schlosser (1939 - 2004)

Biographical: The work of this artist acquired a significant impact on the Spanish art scene during the last quarter of the 20th century. The first years of Schlosser's artistic career were defined by his training in painting at the Graz School, and in sculpture at the Academy of Fine Arts in Vienna. Although Austrian by birth, Schlosser settled in Spain in the mid-1960s, where he lived until his death. Fully incorporated into the generation that in those years marked a new direction for the visual arts in Spain, his contribution to the promotion of new approaches in the field of sculpture was of great importance. After some initial forays into geometric language, Schlosser presented his own vocabulary with a deep relationship with nature. The highlight of this period was the recovery of sculpture as a main form of expression. His growing interest in space and tension led him to experiment with materials such as plastic or methacrylate and rope or elastic band. But in the following years he evolved towards very different forms and materials, beginning to experiment with organic materials extracted directly from nature (wood, stones, mushrooms and later algae, wax, skin, etc.). With these he built a simple, direct, fragile and powerful work in which the nature/culture dichotomy takes shape in a game of tensions. Schlosser can be classified within the group of artists from the Land Art group, whose main characteristic for carrying out their works is inspiration from nature. His efforts earned him official recognition with the National Prize for Visual Arts in 1991.

Joseph Stella (1877-1946)

Biographical: Italian-American artist. His early works were influenced by the Futurist movement, characterized by its fascination with speed, technology and the dynamics of modern life. Stella's Futurist pieces often featured bold colors, dynamic compositions, and a sense of movement. In the 1920s, Stella shifted his focus toward Precisionism, a style that celebrated geometric shapes, clean lines, and the visual aesthetics of industrialization. His Precisionist works often depicted urban scenes, skyscrapers, and bridges in a way that emphasized their precise geometric forms and modernist sensibility. Stella explored various styles and contributed to various art movements, but his fascination with urban landscapes, modernity and the convergence of art and technology remained consistent themes in his work.

In Paris she became acquainted with Carlo Carra, and Giorgio de Chirico, who inspired much of her work. She also came to know Paul Eluard, Max Ernst, Georges Bataille, Henri-Cartier-Bresson, Picasso, Andre Pierre de Mandiarguese, and Salvador Dali. The originality of her art as well as her intelligence, famous wit and charisma accorded her celebrity status in the Paris art world and beyond beginning in the late thirties. She also had a unique talent for film design and created costumes for many films. Her work is in countless public collections such as the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York, Tate Modern in London, Musee d’Art Moderne de la Ville de Paris, Miyazaki Prefectural Art Museum in Japan, and many others. Fini is considered one of the most important women artists of the 20th century.

Jaques Villon (1875-1963)

Biographical: Also known as Gaston Duchamp, was a French Cubist and abstract painter and printmaker. Gaston Duchamp was the elder brother of the artists: Raymond Duchamp-Villon, Marcel Duchamp, and Suzanne Duchamp-Crotti. Villon had established himself as a leading printmaker working within the Belle Époque style long before devoting his considerable talents to painting. He was, with brother Raymond, co-founder of the influential Puteaux Group and he named, and helped stage, the 1912 Salon de la Section d'Or, a seminal exhibition in the history of the Cubism and Orphism movements. His own style of painting was driven by mathematical theory through which he married the flat geometric forms of Cubism with a luminous color palette that saw him referred to by some as the "Cubist-Impressionist". Indeed, his preference for a more expressive palette is credited with helping widen the appeal of Cubism amongst a somewhat sceptical public. As his career developed, he experimented extensively with color theory, and dabbled with pure abstraction, before devoting himself to landscapes and prestigious limited-edition print projects.
